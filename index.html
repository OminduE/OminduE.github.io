Capstone Project · 2025–2026 · University of British Columbia Okanagan
Search & Rescue
Signal Relay System
Communications Engineering · Iterative Prototyping · Systems Design

Project Overview

This capstone project tackled a real communication gap in search and rescue (SAR) operations: when rescuers and ground teams lose line-of-sight (LOS), radio signals degrade and coordination breaks down. Our team designed and prototyped a low-cost, deployable signal relay system intended to maintain communication across terrain obstacles — with a particular focus on iterative hardware development under real-world constraints.

The Problem

In remote terrain — mountain ridges, dense forests, canyon systems — search and rescue teams frequently lose radio contact with base camp as soon as someone crests a hill or enters a valley. This isn't just inconvenient: it's a safety failure. Existing solutions are either too expensive for community SAR teams, too bulky to deploy on foot, or require infrastructure that simply doesn't exist in the wilderness.

Our goal was to prototype something different: a lightweight, deployable relay node that could be carried in a pack, positioned in the field, and re-establish LOS-based communication between two parties who can no longer see each other.

Engineering Approach

We based our system architecture on LOS radio propagation principles. Rather than overcoming terrain with signal power, we chose to physically reposition relay nodes at LOS-clear vantage points — a simpler, more reliable approach for low-budget hardware. The system included a transmitter unit, at least one relay node, and a receiver, with the relay node designed to be quick-deploy and battery-powered.

Iterative Design Process

1

Initial Concept & Benchmarking

We surveyed existing SAR relay solutions and established baseline specs: weight under 2 kg per node, battery life of 4+ hours, and assembly time under 5 minutes. Early sketches explored three form factors: a tripod-mounted unit, a pack-attached module, and a ground-stake design.

2

First Prototype — Hardware Integration

We assembled a first functional prototype using off-the-shelf radio modules and a custom 3D-printed enclosure. The first outdoor test revealed a key issue: the enclosure's geometry blocked the antenna's radiation pattern, causing significant signal loss in the relay direction we needed most.

3

Redesign — Antenna Geometry & Mounting

We restructured the enclosure to expose the antenna externally and added an adjustable tilting mount so the relay node could be aimed precisely. A second round of field tests showed measurable improvement in signal clarity at 150 m.

4

Power & Weatherproofing Challenges

Field testing also revealed that temperature drops reduced battery output more than expected — we had underestimated the power draw of the radio modules in cold conditions. We adjusted the power supply design and added a basic weather seal, though full weatherproofing remained out of scope within our timeline.

Final Outcome & Limitations

What worked
Two-way signal relay achieved at 150 m with LOS vantage positioning. Deployment time met target. Modular enclosure design was functional and field-serviceable.
What didn't
Weatherproofing incomplete. Battery life fell short of the 4-hour target in cold conditions. Non-LOS terrain testing was not fully validated.
Honest assessment
Prototype proved the core concept but is not field-ready. Real deployment would require further hardware iteration and environmental testing.
What comes next
Improved power regulation, ruggedized housing, and extended range testing with directional antennas.

Key Takeaways

  • Prototyping reveals things that models can't — field testing was always more informative than simulation
  • Constraints are design inputs, not obstacles; working within budget and weight limits shaped better decisions
  • Partial success is still engineering progress — knowing what didn't work and why is rigorous, not a failure
  • Communication between team members was as critical as the communication system we were building
  • Every iteration taught something specific; vague testing produces vague learning

Engineering Reflection · Personal Essay
What four years taught me
about building things — and myself

When I started this degree, I thought engineering was mostly about finding the right answer. Four years later, I understand it's really about asking better questions — and being willing to sit with uncertainty long enough to learn something useful from it.

The capstone project made that concrete in a way no course ever did. Our signal relay system didn't fully succeed — battery life fell short, weatherproofing never reached where it needed to be, and some of the terrain tests exposed gaps in our assumptions. But the process of working through those failures, iterating on the design, and understanding exactly why something didn't work? That felt like real engineering. I left that project more confident in my abilities than if everything had gone smoothly. Struggle, it turns out, is where the learning lives.

My co-op at IG Machine & Fibers reinforced a different but equally important lesson: engineering happens inside organizations, not just on drawing boards. I managed projects across maintenance, safety, and process improvement — and quickly realized that the most technically sound solution means nothing if you can't communicate it clearly to the people operating the equipment every day. Writing SOPs, running team briefings, documenting design decisions — those skills aren't soft. They're how engineering actually gets implemented.

I've also come to see engineers as translators between technical possibility and human need. We don't build for buildings — we build for the people who use them, maintain them, and depend on them. That responsibility matters to me.

In terms of growth: I'm strongest when I can get my hands on something physical, iterate quickly, and work in a team with a clear shared problem. I'm still developing my comfort with open-ended, poorly-defined challenges — but I've learned that sitting with ambiguity is a skill, and one I've been actively building since first year.

I graduate not as someone who has figured it out, but as someone who finally knows how to figure things out. That distinction feels like the whole point.
