Project Summary

Quick-scan cards so the page feels like a portfolio, not a report.

Problem

Search and rescue teams can lose reliable communication as aircraft move deeper into mountainous or forested terrain.

Solution

A relay UAV concept designed to act as a support platform between the operator and the main drone.

My Role

CAD design, prototyping, assembly support, and design iteration based on testing outcomes.

Tools

SolidWorks, 3D printing, electronics integration, bench testing, and flight-readiness iteration.

Design Process

Use clear steps and visuals to tell the story.

1

Role definition and mission selection

The team initially explored several UAV roles, then focused on relay capability after client feedback made it clear that communication support was the most valuable need.

2

Configuration tradeoff

A fixed-wing aircraft offered endurance, while a quadcopter offered easy deployment. Hermes moved toward a hybrid VTOL approach to combine both strengths.

3

Prototype testing and redesign

Early test iterations exposed lift and airflow issues, which led to a redesign of the wing extender geometry and motor positioning.

4

Final validation and project limits

The redesigned aircraft achieved stable quadcopter flight, but fixed-wing transition validation remained limited by a rear propulsion issue and overall timeline constraints.

Results

Short cards work better here than long paragraphs.

What worked

The redesigned geometry improved flight stability and demonstrated that the relay UAV concept was technically feasible.

Main issue

A rear propulsion limitation prevented full transition validation within the capstone timeline.

Value added

The project responded directly to a real client need and showed that a lower-cost support platform could fit existing workflows.

Next step

Improve the tail propulsion system, continue transition testing, and refine the platform for more robust field operation.

Key Takeaways

These make good closing points for readers and recruiters.

Iteration mattered more than getting the first design “right.” Each test directly informed the next version of the aircraft.
The project balanced performance, portability, cost, and feasibility rather than chasing an unrealistic final product.
Client feedback shaped the mission direction early, which made the final concept much more relevant and defensible.
Even partial validation was valuable because it clarified exactly where the design succeeded and where future work is still needed.

Engineering Reflection

What four years taught me about building things — and myself

I graduate not as someone who has figured everything out, but as someone who has learned how to work through uncertainty and build toward better answers.

When I started engineering, I thought success mostly meant finding the right answer. Over time, I learned that the better skill is learning how to ask stronger questions, test assumptions, and improve through iteration.

Capstone made that lesson real. Not everything worked perfectly, but the value came from understanding why, redesigning what we could, and making the next version better. That process gave me more confidence than an easy success would have.

My co-op experience reinforced something else important: good engineering does not stop at technical quality. A design only matters if it can be communicated clearly, implemented properly, and understood by the people who will actually use it.

I’m strongest when I can combine hands-on work with structured problem solving. Going forward, that is the kind of engineering environment I want to keep building in.
